# 01 — Vision & Pitch

## High-Level Pitch

**Dead Letter Office** is a first-person narrative job-sim where the player works the night shift in an isolated postal archive. Their task is to process, inspect, sort, archive, destroy, or deliver undeliverable mail from a forgotten town called **Marrow Creek**.

At first, the job feels mundane and bureaucratic. The player reads names, checks addresses, matches stamps, verifies dates, and sorts letters according to official rules.

Over time, the letters begin to contradict each other.

- Some letters are dated years before the sender was born.
- Some are addressed to dead people.
- Some describe events that have not happened yet.
- Some contain hidden codes, invisible ink, strange stains, torn photographs, audio fragments, and newspaper clippings.
- Eventually, some letters are addressed directly to the player.

The office itself begins changing in subtle ways. Filing cabinets move. The town map updates. New rooms appear. Old policy manuals rewrite themselves. The player realizes the office is not just storing mail. It is controlling what is remembered, what is forgotten, and who is allowed to exist.

This is not a generic horror walking simulator. The player always has a job to do, rules to follow, systems to interact with, secrets to uncover, and unsettling contradictions to notice.

## Steam Capsule Hook

**Sort undeliverable mail from a dead town where every letter changes the truth, and some letters are addressed to you.**

Alternative hooks:

- A haunted postal job-sim about forgotten people, impossible letters, and a town erased from memory.
- Read, sort, destroy, or deliver letters from a town that officially never existed.
- Papers, Please meets psychological horror in a dead-letter archive full of impossible mail.

## Genre

Primary genre:

- Narrative mystery job-sim

Secondary genres:

- Psychological horror
- Puzzle investigation
- Atmospheric first-person adventure
- Bureaucratic horror
- Light document detective game

Comparable vibes — not direct clones:

- Papers, Please
- The Return of the Obra Dinn
- Stories Untold
- The Mortuary Assistant
- Her Story
- Anatomy
- Inscryption's hidden-lore feeling
- Control's bureaucratic weirdness
- The Stanley Parable's uncanny office atmosphere

The game should not imitate these directly. The goal is to capture the feeling of "a simple task becomes increasingly disturbing."

## Target Player

The target player enjoys:

- Mystery games
- Horror without constant jumpscares
- Reading and decoding clues
- Dark atmosphere
- Hidden lore
- Secrets and easter eggs
- Short but memorable indie games
- Games streamers can theorize about
- Games that reward observation
- Games where the world slowly becomes stranger

They do **not** need to enjoy:

- Combat
- Fast reflexes
- Multiplayer
- Massive open worlds
- Grinding
- Survival crafting
- Complex RPG systems

## Design Pillars

Everything in the game should support these pillars.

### Pillar 1: The Job Comes First

The player is always doing a real task. They are not just wandering around waiting for spooky sounds. They have mail to inspect, sort, stamp, classify, archive, reject, or deliver.

The horror comes from noticing that the job itself is wrong.

### Pillar 2: Every Letter Matters

Every letter should reveal something. A letter can reveal:

- A relationship
- A lie
- A timeline contradiction
- A death
- A hidden location
- A code
- A future event
- A town secret
- Something about the player

No letter should feel like meaningless filler.

### Pillar 3: Secrets Are Layered

The player should be able to finish the game casually, but deeper players should uncover hidden truths. Secrets exist in layers:

- Obvious story clues
- Optional character connections
- Hidden codes
- Environmental changes
- Repeated symbols
- Secret endings
- ARG-like details, but not required to enjoy the game

### Pillar 4: The Office Is Alive, But Subtle

The office should change slowly. Examples:

- A filing cabinet appears open when it was closed.
- A stamp changes text.
- The wall map gains a street.
- A clock skips backward.
- A policy manual gains a new paragraph.
- A bin label changes from "Archive" to "Confess."
- A letter appears on the player's desk without being delivered.

No cheap monster hallway nonsense unless carefully earned.

### Pillar 5: The Player Makes Meaningful Choices

The player should make choices with consequences. Choices include:

- Deliver a letter or archive it
- Destroy evidence or preserve it
- Follow office rules or disobey
- Report contradictions or hide them
- Trust the official system or the people in the letters
- Learn the truth or keep the town buried

Choices should affect:

- Which lore is revealed
- Which characters are remembered
- Which rooms unlock
- Which ending occurs
- How the office reacts

## Final Creative Direction

The game should feel lonely, tense, intimate, and strange.

The horror should come from:

- Contradictions
- Lost people
- Cold systems
- Memory erasure
- Impossible mail
- The player's growing implication in the mystery

The emotional core:

> People disappear twice: once when they die, and again when nobody remembers them.

Dead Letter Office is about refusing to let people disappear the second time.

The final game should make players feel like they are not just solving a mystery. They are deciding who deserves to be remembered.

## One-Sentence North Star

**Dead Letter Office is a dark postal mystery where every undeliverable letter is a person asking not to be forgotten.**
