# 11 — Marketing & Commercial Strategy

## Trailer Design

The trailer must sell the game in 60 to 90 seconds.

### First 10 Seconds

- Close-up of a letter being stamped "UNDELIVERABLE."
- The ink spreads like blood.
- Player opens the letter.
- The letter reads: "If you are reading this, the town still exists."
- Cut to wall map: Marrow Creek appears.

### Trailer Beats

1. Normal sorting job.
2. Inspecting names, dates, addresses.
3. First contradiction: recipient died years before letter was sent.
4. Office rule: "Destroy all mail addressed to deceased persons."
5. Player chooses to archive instead.
6. Office lights flicker.
7. Map changes.
8. Black envelope appears.
9. Letter addressed to "Current Clerk."
10. Fast cuts of: incinerator, filing cabinets, redacted file, phone ringing, delivery chute, children's drawing, letter dated tomorrow.
11. Final line: "Some letters should never be delivered."
12. Title card: **Dead Letter Office**

The trailer must communicate:

- What the player does
- Why it becomes creepy
- What the mystery is
- Why choices matter

Do not make a vague horror trailer with random screaming. That is how you join the landfill.

## Streamer / YouTube / TikTok Potential

The game should create moments like:

- "Wait, this kid died before he wrote this."
- "Chat, should I burn this letter?"
- "The map just changed."
- "That letter is addressed to me."
- "The handwriting is the same."
- "The office manual changed."
- "This room was not here before."
- "Why is there a photo of my desk?"
- "The stamp says CONFESS now."
- "The dead kid is answering."

Streamer-friendly features:

- Clear choices
- Readable mysteries
- Chat can help decode clues
- Multiple endings
- Secret hunting
- Short enough for one or two streams
- Creepy but not constantly loud
- Strong thumbnail moments

TikTok-friendly clips:

- Impossible letter reveals
- Office changing
- Hidden ink messages
- Black envelope opening
- Delivery chute consequences
- Player-name reveal, if implemented carefully
- Creepy phone call moments

## Market Positioning

Sell the game as:

- A dark postal job-sim
- A mystery about impossible letters
- A psychological horror game without cheap jumpscares
- A short, polished, story-rich experience
- A game full of secrets and hidden lore

Steam tags: Mystery, Psychological Horror, Narrative, Investigation, Puzzle, Atmospheric, Singleplayer, Story Rich, Dark, First-Person, Choices Matter, Detective, Hidden Object (maybe), Simulation (maybe), Horror (but not only horror).

Avoid positioning it as:

- Pure horror walking simulator
- Pure visual novel
- Pure puzzle game
- Cozy mail sim
- Survival horror
- Roguelike
- Open-world mystery

## Price Point

- Demo: free
- Full game: $9.99 to $14.99

Guidance:

- If the game is 2 hours: $9.99
- If the game is 3 to 5 hours with multiple endings: $14.99
- If production value is very high: $16.99 possible, but risky for a first indie game

Discount strategy:

- Launch discount: 10%
- Seasonal sales: 20% to 35%
- Avoid racing to 80% off early

## Commercial Strategy

### Steam Page

Launch the Steam page as soon as the game has:

- Strong capsule art
- 6 to 8 screenshots
- 1 short teaser trailer
- Clear description
- GIFs of letter sorting and office changes

The Steam page should focus on: sorting mail, impossible letters, the erased town, choices, secrets, multiple endings.

### Demo

The demo should end on a strong hook. Ideal demo ending:

The player finishes Shift 2. They destroy or archive Jonah Bell's impossible letter. The lights go out. A new letter appears.

> **Recipient:** Current Clerk
> **Date:** Tomorrow
> **Message:** "You found me too early."

End demo. Wishlist screen.

### Marketing Clips

Create clips showing:

- Stamping letters
- Hidden ink reveal
- Map changing
- Burned letter returning
- Letter addressed to player
- Rule manual rewriting itself
- Phone call from Jonah
- Black envelope opening

### Influencer Pitch

Send the demo to streamers with this pitch:

> "Dead Letter Office is a short psychological mystery where you sort undeliverable mail from a town that does not exist. Every letter can reveal hidden lore, contradict official records, or change the story depending on what you do with it."

## What Makes This Game Worth Building

This idea is commercially promising because it has:

- A simple core action
- A strong mystery hook
- A contained environment
- Low asset burden
- Strong writing potential
- Great streamer moments
- Easy trailer readability
- Lots of room for secrets
- Clear Steam capsule identity
- Expandable lore
- No multiplayer risk
- No giant open-world scope
- No expensive combat system

It is realistic because most of the game can be built from: letters, UI, one office, sound design, lighting, state changes, and strong writing.

The game does not need massive graphics. It needs taste, atmosphere, pacing, and restraint.

## Main Risks

### Risk 1: Too Much Reading

**Solution:** Keep letters short. Mix reading with physical interaction, inspection, sorting, environmental changes, and audio events.

### Risk 2: Too Confusing

**Solution:** Track clues clearly. Let casual players understand the main story while deeper secrets remain optional.

### Risk 3: Boring Office Gameplay

**Solution:** Make the sorting loop tactile, fast, and satisfying. Add new tools and rules each shift.

### Risk 4: Weak Writing

**Solution:** Write fewer letters, but make each one matter. Every letter should reveal character, contradiction, or consequence.

### Risk 5: Over-Scoping

**Solution:** Do not build the town as an explorable world in version 1. The town exists through documents, map changes, sounds, and imagination. That is cheaper and scarier.
